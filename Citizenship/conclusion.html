Implicit Biases

     Awareness of implicit biases is the first step toward treating everyone equally. The second step requires vigilance to recognize when these implicit biases creep into our lives and influence our behavior and decisions. It also requires consistent willpower to reshape our attitudes and behaviors in light of this recognition. As testament to this claim, consider a study published in 2007 of referees for NBA games from 1991-2004. The primary function of any referee is to apply the rules fairly to all players and obey the principle of equal treatment. However, the study found that referees do operate according to implicit racial bias: "[p]layers earn up to 4% fewer fouls or score up to 2.5% more points when they are the recipients of a positive own-race bias."

     More relevant to our claim that consciousness of implicit biases can promote equal treatment, the authors revisited their findings in a 2013 study titled Awareness Reduces Racial Bias. The authors show that these implicit racial biases persisted until 2007 when the issue was raised to the sphere of public awareness. This media attention applied pressure to the NBA to conduct racial bias training for referees. The study found that after implicit bias training, referees no longer demonstrated any statistically significant effects of racial bias. This finding supports our claim that personal awareness and honest diligence is a useful antidote for implicit biases. This awareness may be effective for promoting equality and treating everyone fairly.
