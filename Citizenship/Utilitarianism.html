Utilitarianism

     Utilitarianism seeks to maximize happiness and minimize unhappiness. Bentham's view of Utilitarianism was to only restrict actions that may be harmful to others; in other words, citizens in a Utilitarian society have freedom to do as they please as long as it causes no harm. It's worth noting that this system seeking to maximize happiness is primarily reserved for humans (due to their ability to reason). Thus, if bulldozing the last remaining habitat of an endangered species to put a shopping mall in its place would provide more happiness to humans then this should be done.

     While Compassionate Critical Thinking champions happiness, personal liberty and freedom of ideas, it does not contend that the world will be a better place as a result of maximizing human happiness. Compassionate Critical Thinking also recognizes inalienable rights of not only humans, but all living things. It does not attempt to evaluate the consequences of restricted freedom of actions by weighing them against the units of happiness or unhappiness that may be produced for humans alone. Personal freedom and happiness is important to Compassionate Critical Thinking but it also recognizes that individual rights of living things (which may restrict the personal liberties of others) also have substantial value.
