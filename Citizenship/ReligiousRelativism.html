Religious Relativism

     "Is conduct right because the gods command it, or do the gods command it because it's right?" This question frames the "relative" aspect of Religious Relativism and neatly divides Religious morality into two general classifications: Divine Command Theory and the Theory of Natural Law.

     Under Divine Command Theory, divine laws are subject to interpretation of "God's will" and they suffer from the same arbitrary relativity of beliefs as Cultural Relativism (different perspectives maintain different views of "right" and "wrong"). Additionally, religious adherents often accept interpretations of "God's will" as final, unquestionable, and beyond reproach on the basis of God's authority; this authority grants religious beliefs their "special," privileged nature. Consequently, actions are "right" if they can be justified by religious dogma, irrespective of any actual harm that they may inflict upon living things. As such, piety requires neither sincere compassion nor open-minded critical thinking (and, at times, may be at odds with these principles).

     Compassionate Critical Thinking makes no claims about the natural order or purpose of things according to divine intention or natural law. Thus, Compassionate Critical Thinking is at odds with the Theory of Natural Law. Instead of viewing the world as an anthropocentric pyramid that places human interests above all else, the world is seen as an interrelated web of living things whose interests should not be marginalized merely for their inability to communicate them. Moreover, it does not contend that perfect recognition of Divine or Natural Law provides definitive guidelines about how all humans ought to conduct themselves.
